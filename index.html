About the Lab

Where Biology Meets Bioelectricity

The Ratnam Neurophysiology Laboratory at Ahmedabad University is dedicated to understanding how nervous systems generate, encode, and interpret signals from the environment. We work across multiple model systems — weakly electric fish (Apteronotus leptorhynchus), chorusing frogs and toads, songbirds, and humans — to uncover conserved and divergent principles of neural computation.

"Biological mechanisms, which are a result of natural selection, reach theoretical performance bounds predicted by mathematical and engineering analysis. Selection and optimal design are one and the same." — Prof. Rama Ratnam

A core theme is the idea that evolution has converged to neural codes operating near the theoretical limits of performance — codes that are both high-fidelity and energy-efficient. This principle has profound implications for understanding brain function and for developing next-generation cochlear implants and neural prosthetics.

What We Study

Research Focus Areas

See Publications →

Electrosensory Neurophysiology

Studying how weakly electric fish (Apteronotus leptorhynchus, wave-type electric fish) encode and process electric organ discharges — a model system for active sensing circuits and optimal neural coding. A core aim is testing the source-coding hypothesis: that neurons minimize energy while maximizing coding fidelity.

Optimal Neural Coding Theory

A neuron operating at fixed metabolic energy must trade off energy against coding fidelity. Prof. Ratnam & collaborators show that a single potassium M-current is the key biophysical regulator of this optimal code — producing spike-timing statistics that maximize signal detection and estimation performance.

Anuran Vocal Communication

Investigating male-male interactions in chorusing frogs — how callers synchronize, alternate antiphonally, and track neighbors using phase-hopping. Using microphone array beamforming to localize and separate individual frog voices in the dark, and studying how frog auditory midbrain neurons detect signals in noise.

Sound Localization & Auditory Neuroethology

Exploring how human listeners process and localize complex acoustic signals — including synthetic narrowband frog calls. Research by lab member Hemal Faizpurkar shows systematic human errors in locating frog calls, revealing evolutionary pressures shaping vocal communication signal design.

Cochlear Implant Speech Coding

Applying the optimal neural spike-timing code to develop improved speech vocoders for cochlear implants. Preliminary results show improved binaural and directional hearing in vocoder simulations. Particularly relevant for India's large pediatric hearing-loss population, where cochlear implant penetration remains low.

EEG, Task-Load & Human Performance

Multimodal physiological recordings (ERP/EEG, eye-tracking, galvanic skin response) to assess cognitive task-load in work environments. Combining these signals in linear regression models to predict performance under varying task demand — with implications for human-computer interaction and workplace safety.

What You'll Learn

  • In-vivo extracellular electrophysiology in weakly electric fish (P-type afferents, ELL recordings)

  • Speech vocoder design & cochlear implant simulation
  • Otoacoustic emissions (OAE) & audiometry
  • MATLAB / Python signal processing & computational modelling
  • Information theory & statistical neuroscience
